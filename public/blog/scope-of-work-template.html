Scope of Work Template for Freelancers (2025)

Scope creep costs the average freelancer 12–20% of their annual income. A well-written scope of work document fixes that before the project even starts. Here's the template, a real example, and what to write in each section.

What Is a Scope of Work (SOW)?

A scope of work is a document that defines exactly what will and will not be delivered in a project. It's not a contract (though it's often attached to one) — it's a shared definition of reality that both you and your client agree on before work begins.

The goal of a good SOW isn't just to protect yourself legally. It's to prevent the conversations you dread: "Can you just add one more page?" "I thought revisions were unlimited." "I assumed email marketing was included."

A great SOW makes those conversations unnecessary because the answers are already in writing, agreed to upfront.

When Do You Need a Scope of Work?

Always — but especially for:

  • Projects over $1,000 in total value
  • Projects with multiple phases or milestones
  • Projects where the client is unclear about what they want
  • Any project where revision rounds could spiral
  • Long-term retainer agreements

For small, well-defined tasks under $500 (e.g., "fix this bug"), a brief email confirming the deliverable and price is often enough. For everything else, an SOW is non-negotiable.

The 6 Sections Every SOW Needs

Section 1: Project Overview

A 2–3 sentence summary of the project: what the client needs, why, and what success looks like. This isn't just filler — it ensures you and the client have the same understanding of the goal before diving into specifics.

Section 2: Deliverables

A numbered list of specific, named outputs. The key word is specific:

  • ❌ "Logo design"
  • ✓ "Primary logo in SVG and PNG formats, including horizontal and vertical lockups, with a color and monochrome version — 8 files total"

Vague deliverables are where scope creep begins. If a deliverable can be interpreted two ways, assume the client will interpret it to their advantage.

Section 3: What's NOT Included

This is the section most freelancers skip, and it's the most valuable one for preventing disputes.

List every adjacent service or deliverable that a client might reasonably assume is included, but isn't. For a branding project, that might be:

  • Website design or development
  • Copywriting or tagline development
  • Print production or file preparation for print vendors
  • Social media templates or marketing collateral
  • Brand photography or illustration

Writing this section takes 5 minutes and can save 5 hours of awkward renegotiation later.

Section 4: Timeline and Milestones

Break the project into phases with specific durations or completion dates. Include:

  • When you need input or approvals from the client (and what happens if they're delayed)
  • Key milestones where work pauses for review before proceeding
  • The final delivery date and what it assumes (no delays in client feedback)

Section 5: Revision Policy

Define exactly how many rounds of revisions are included and what counts as a "revision" vs. a new request. A common structure:

  • 2 rounds of revisions included per deliverable
  • Additional revisions billed at $[X]/hour or $[X]/round
  • A "revision" means changes to existing work. New directions or additional deliverables are quoted separately.

Section 6: Client Responsibilities

What do you need from the client to do your job? List it explicitly:

  • Access to existing brand assets, accounts, or systems
  • Content or copy provided by the client (by what date)
  • Feedback within [X] business days of each review
  • A single point of contact for approvals

This section is especially important for protecting your timeline. If the project runs late because the client didn't provide content on time, your SOW protects you from being held responsible.

A Real SOW Example (Web Design Project)

Example SOW — Web Design Project

Project Overview

This project covers the redesign of Bloom Skincare's Shopify storefront, with the goal of improving mobile conversion rate and reducing checkout abandonment. Work begins after receipt of 50% deposit and signed agreement.

Deliverables

  1. UX audit report (PDF, 8–12 pages) with CRO recommendations
  2. Figma prototype — 6 page templates, mobile and desktop breakpoints
  3. Custom Shopify theme build based on approved Figma designs
  4. Product page template with sticky cart, review integration, and urgency module
  5. Optimized checkout flow (1-page checkout)
  6. QA report confirming testing across Chrome, Safari, Firefox on mobile and desktop
  7. 2-week post-launch bug fix support

What's NOT Included

  • Product photography, video, or copywriting
  • Klaviyo, email marketing, or SMS configuration
  • Product data entry or inventory migration
  • Shopify app subscriptions (client's responsibility)
  • Ongoing maintenance or hosting after 2-week support window

Timeline

  • Phase 1: Discovery & Audit — 5 business days
  • Phase 2: Figma design — 8 business days (pending client feedback within 3 days of each review)
  • Phase 3: Development — 10 business days
  • Phase 4: QA & Launch — 3 business days

Timeline assumes timely client feedback. Delays of more than 5 business days in client approvals may extend the project timeline proportionally.

Revisions

2 rounds of revisions included per deliverable phase. Additional revisions are billed at $120/hour. A revision is defined as changes to approved work. New directions or significant scope additions will be quoted separately.

SOW vs. Contract: What's the Difference?

A scope of work defines what you'll deliver. A contract defines the terms under which you deliver it — payment schedule, IP ownership, dispute resolution, etc.

For most freelance projects, you need both. A common approach is to have a short master services agreement (MSA) that covers the legal terms, and attach the SOW as an exhibit. The SOW is project-specific; the MSA covers your standard working terms across all projects.

If you only have one document, make sure it covers at minimum: deliverables, payment terms, revision policy, and what happens if either party needs to cancel.
